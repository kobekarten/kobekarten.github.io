What Makes Something a Religion?

There is no universally agreed definition of “religion” in philosophy, sociology, or law. Two broad approaches dominate the debate:

  • Substantive definitions focus on what a religion is: belief in supernatural entities, sacred texts, and a community of adherents.
  • Functional definitions focus on what a religion does: providing ultimate meaning, community, a moral framework, and ways of coping with finitude.

Pastafarianism satisfies many criteria on both approaches. Whether that is sufficient — given its explicitly parodic origin — is the central question of this session.

The Sincere Belief Argument

Martin (2020) argues that the decisive criterion is sincerity: a belief system qualifies as a religion for legal purposes if its adherents sincerely hold its tenets and are prepared to bear real costs for those beliefs. On this view, the origin of the belief system (parodic or otherwise) is irrelevant; only the current attitude of the believer matters.
